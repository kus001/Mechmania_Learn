:::caution
Electrical systems can be dangerous if handled incorrectly. Always verify and be aware of voltage, polarity, and component ratings before connecting a circuit.
:::

Before starting to design circuits or wiring robots, it is important to understand the basic measurements used to describe electricity to know what you are working with.

The 3 basic measurements are:

- Voltage
- Current
- Resistance

We will dive into each of these measurements.

## Voltage

Voltage is the electrical push or force that drives current through a circuit. It is measured in **Volts (V)**.

More precisely, voltage represents **electric potential difference**. It is a measurement of how much energy is required to move a unit of electric charge from one point to another in a circuit. 

:::danger
Feeding higher voltage into a component than it is rated for will **damage** the component.
:::

## Current

Current is the rate at which electric charge flows through a circuit. It is measured in **Amperes or Amps (A)**. It measures how much electric charge flows through the circuit each second.

:::note
Components draw current based on their electrical load. Your battery/power supply must be able to safely supply the current your system demands.

For example: if a power supply is rated for **4A**, but your circuit only demands **2A**, the power supply will only output **2A**. However, your power supply must be capable of supplying at least the total current your circuit requires.
:::

## Resistance

Resistance opposes the flow of electric current through a circuit. It is measured in **Ohms (Ω)**. It restricts how much current can pass through the circuit.

**Why Do We Use Resistance?**

We intentionally add components called **resistors** to limit the amount of current passing through a circuit branch. Without resistance:
- A component like an **LED** would draw too much current and instantly explode or burn out.
- A circuit could short, creating dangerously high current that melts components or wiring.

## Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law connects Voltage, Current, and Resistance into a single formula. It is the most fundamental equation in electronics:

`V = I × R`

By rearranging this formula, you can calculate any missing value in your circuit:

- **Calculate Voltage:** `V = I × R`
- **Calculate Current:** `I = V / R`
- **Calculate Resistance:** `R = V / I`

### The Relationship Between V, I, and R

Ohm's law is the mathematical representation of the relationship between the three variables.

We can use it to find theoretical values for our circuits, for example, given a voltage and resistance, we can apply `I = V / R` to find the current.

We can also use it to observe how affecting one variable affects the other two:
For example, if **Resistance (R)** stays constant, increasing **Voltage (V)** will cause **Current (I)** to increase proportionally.

In the same measure, should we want the current to stay constant, then we would have to increase the resistance until `R = V / I`.


## Example

Suppose a motor rated for **12V** has an internal resistance of **6Ω**. We connect it to a **12V power supply**.

Using Ohm's Law to find how much current the motor will draw:

`I = V / R`  
`I = 12V / 6Ω`  
`I = 2A`  

At its 12V rating, the motor will draw **2 Amps** of current to operate.
